Spartacus

The lowly slave who almost toppled the mighty Roman Empire

Short Biography

Organised and led an ad hoc group of slaves into a series of consecutive military victories against the vast, professional and better resourced Roman army. By doing so, Spartacus brazenly and powerfully struck at the very epicentre of the largest empire of the ancient world almost bringing it to its knees. During a chaotic two year period of the uprising, fundamental questions around basic human rights issues such as equality, justice and freedom circulated in a way that had never previously been possible, offering hope to many oppressed people. The Roman empire was forced into being extremely careful in its management of slaves and its conquered lands following these experiences.

Time-line of Spartacus:

(Note: many of the following details are contested owing to the uncertainty of ancient historical sources.)

  • 111BC - Born in Thrace (modern day Bulgaria).
  • 90 BC - Marries a prophetess from the same Maedi tribe.
  • 80 BC - Served in the Roman army as an auxillary.
  • 76 BC - Sold into slavery after desertion.
  • 74 BC - Arrives as a slave at the gladiatorial school in Capua, Northern Italy.
  • 73 BC - Escapes with about 70 others after over powering guards and leads slaves into the Third Servile War with success at the inital confrontation against Commander Glaber's forces at Mount Vesuvius.
  • 72 BC - Heads south towards the tip of Italy. Defeats Roman legions of Gellius and Clodianus in many battles. Breaks through Roman lines across mainland Italy as slave army marches as far north as Gaul (modern day France).
  • 71 BC - Turns around and heads south back towards Italy to meet the Romans head on. Died on the battlefield of Lucania as rebel force is defeated by a hugely fortified army led by General Crassus.

Legacy

The efforts and achievements of Spartacus has been used as a symbol and inspiration for oppressed people throughout history as a motivating force to shake up an unjust system. For instance, the revolutionary spirit was invoked by leaders of the successful Haitian independence movement against French colonisers during the 1790â€™s. As such, Spartacus is regarded as a hero for the downtrodden because he is thought to embody a deep-seated sense of struggle against tyranny and subjugation. This he did by demonstrating unflinching persistence and the utility of guerrilla tactics against a better equipped force. Unsuprisingly, the sheer compelling nature of this historical narrative has ensured Spartacus has become a major figure within popular culture. Books, poems and musical performances have all celebrated the achievements and courage of the slave revolt. Additionally, Stanley Kubrick's 1960 Hollywood epic introduced the figure to a wider audience as have two recent television series. The bravery, solidarity and heroism exuded by the slaves has resulted in many sports teams around the world adopting the name of Spartacus to denote the values of their club and commitment to their cause.
